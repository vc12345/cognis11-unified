Module 47: Valid Cube Nets

Phase 1: The X-Ray

Think of a cube net as a "Flattened Cardboard Box." In the 11+, the biggest hurdle is mentally folding that 2D shape back into a 3D solid. The secret weapon? Identifying Opposite Pairs. Because opposite faces (like the Lid and Base) can never touch each other and can never be seen at the same time, finding them is the fastest way to spot impossible decoy answers. Use the Leapfrog Rule: in any straight line of three or more squares, the faces separated by exactly one "jump" are partners.

The Standard Cross Identify the "Base" face if the "Top" face is selected in a 1-4-1 net.
The T-Shape Find all three opposite pairs in a standard T-style layout.
The Advanced "Z" Solve a "Z-Net" where there are no long straight lines of three squares.

Phase 4: The Saboteur

The "Touching Corner" Trap: Most children assume that if two squares share a corner on a flat sheet of paper, they must touch on the cube. This is an illusion! In the 3D world, many faces that look far apart on the net actually fold to meet at the exact same edge.

The Insider Fix: The "Opposite Shield." Tell your child: "Don't look for what DOES touch; look for what CAN'T." Find the three opposite pairs first. If a 3D answer shows two of those opposites sharing a line or a corner, that answer is a scientific impossibility. Cross it out and move on—no second-guessing needed.
