Foundation Intro

The Fair Share Value

Imagine three close friends hold completely different amounts of party candy. The Fair Share Value is the exact number of sweets everyone receives if they pool all their candy together into one big mountain and then redistribute it perfectly equally.

"Look closely at our mini candy piles below: we have 5, 1, and 6. That gives us 12 pieces of candy in total. Click the 'Make it Fair!' button and watch the sweets shift. Since there are 3 friends sharing, we distribute the 12 sweets so that everyone holds exactly 4. That shared value of 4 is our Fair Share answer!"

Foundation Progressing

The Height Parade (The Line-Up Middle)

The Line-Up Middle is simply the value belonging to the exact middle person standing in a queue. But there is a vital rule: the group must stand in strict height order! If the numbers are jumbled up out of sequence, the person standing in the middle spot will not give you the true answer.

"Look at this jumbled row of heights: 8, 2, 5, 9, 3. Who is currently occupying the middle spot? It looks like 5. But wait! Click 'Line Up in Order!'. Now that they are arranged properly from shortest to tallest, you can see the true middle person balances at 5. Jumbled lists are designed to trick your eyes!"
Heights: 8, 2, 5, 9, 3. Stand in order to find the middle!

Phase 1: Spotting the Pattern

Think of tracking midpoints like finding the clean "Center of Gravity" for a collection of data. In competitive 11+ exams, this data family contains three main siblings: the Fair Share Value (the perfect equalizer), the Line-Up Middle (the physical midpoint of an ordered queue), and the Popularity Winner (the most frequent value). Success depends entirely on choosing the right tool for the specific story context provided.

The Fair Share: "Find the fair share midpoint of 3, 5, and 7. If these numbers are shared out completely equally, what value does each share hold?"
The Line-Up Middle: "Find the middle marker for the list 15, 4, 9, 15, 2. Which value rests in the exact physical center when ordered?"
The Missing Entry: "The fair share value of four children's ages is exactly 12. Three of the kids are 10, 11, and 14 years old. How old is the fourth child?"

Phase 4: Common Mistake to Watch For

Phase 4: Common Mistake to Watch For

The "Jumbled Middle" Speed Trap: Rushed children look at an unsorted list like "8, 2, 5" and instantly pick 2 simply because it sits physically in the middle of the typed text line. This is an exam disaster! The middle value is meaningless unless the row is organized.

The Easy Fix: The "Height Parade" Rule. Remind your child: "Numbers must always stand in height order from shortest to tallest." Never select a midpoint value until your list is sorted securely. If you have an even number of entries with no single center person, take the fair share average of the two middle values.
