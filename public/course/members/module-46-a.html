The Spotlight Method

In hard puzzles, there are too many things moving at once. To win, you must act like a spotlight—look at only one thing at a time and ignore the rest. This is the "Pinch Method."

"This box is messy! There is a dot, a square, and a cross. Don't stare at the whole thing. Click 'Focus on Dot'. See how everything else disappears? Now you can easily see the rule for the dot without getting confused."

Phase 1: The X-Ray

Think of this as "The Detective's Film Strip." The secret weapon is finding the Delta—the mathematical rule of change between frames. If an object jumps in step 1, it must repeat that vector in the final step. Track just one tiny piece of the film at a time to avoid cognitive overload.

Single Jump "A circle moves top-left to bottom-right. Where will it land next?"
Dual Rule "A shape rotates 90° clockwise AND flips color in each step."
Triple Threat "Object changes position, shading, and size at once. Track them separately!"

Phase 2: The Anatomy

The Vector Scan. Notice how elements move independently. Each "character" has its own script. When isolated, the complex dance becomes a predictable sequence. Use the engine below to toggle different logic scripts.

Phase 3: The Engine

Select a logic type. Watch the Trace Log identify the "Deltas" (change vectors). The answer is found in the parts, not the whole.

Phase 4: The Saboteur

The "Visual Overload" Trap: Children try to "feel" the answer by staring at the whole frame. The brain gets overwhelmed by moving parts and misses the critical detail.

The Fix: The "Pinch" Method. "Divide and conquer." Physically cover everything except for one element. Solve that dot first. Then ignore it and move to the next. **Three simple puzzles are easier than one big one.**
