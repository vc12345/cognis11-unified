Foundation Intro

The Land of Both

Overlapping diagrams work just like standard sorting baskets, but with a clever twist. Imagine sorting things where some items fit into a Green basket and others fit into a Small basket. But what happens if an item is both Green AND Small at the same time? It cannot sit in two distant places at once, so we overlap the loops to create a shared middle space called The Shared Zone.

The Shared Doorway

In competitive 11+ papers, surveyed groups almost always have a sneaky overlap. If Group A contains 10 people total, but 3 of those individuals are also members of Group B, then Group A only has 7 people who belong ONLY to Group A. We must slide the circles together and subtract that shared middle group to see the true values.

"Imagine two adjoining classrooms. Room A holds 15 children. However, 5 of them are actually sitting right in the middle doorway, sharing space with Room B. If we do not subtract those 5 from our separate room counts, we accidentally count them twice and our overall total breaks! Watch how our 'Only' numbers change when the rooms slide together."

Phase 1: Spotting the Pattern

Think of sorting diagrams as "The Land of Both." Your main tool for unlocking these puzzles is The Shared Zone—the overlapping sweet spot where items fulfill both rules at the exact same time. Blindly adding two separate group totals together sets a dangerous "double counting" trap. To find the absolute truth, always carve the shared overlap away from the grand totals to identify who sits "Only" inside a single loop.

The School Sports Club: "A club contains 12 football players, but 3 of these players also play rugby. How many children play football ONLY?"
The Overlapping Merge: "Group A has a total of 20 members, and Group B has 15 members. If 5 members belong to both, what is the count of unique individuals?"
The Minimum Boundary: "Out of 40 students, 25 like Art class and 20 like Music class. What is the smallest possible number of students who must like both subjects?"

Phase 4: Common Mistake to Watch For

Phase 4: Common Mistake to Watch For

The "Blind Addition" Speed Trap: Rushed students scan a problem, see "15 Footballers" and "10 Swimmers," and instantly add them to say "25 people total." If 5 of those kids do both activities, the student has accidentally conjured 5 imaginary people out of thin air!

The Easy Fix: The "Count Once Only" Rule. Teach your child to clean up the groups by subtracting the overlap value from both side counts first. Isolate your clean outer groups before doing any addition. The true total formula follows a clean balance line: Total = (Group A - Overlap) + (Group B - Overlap) + Overlap.
