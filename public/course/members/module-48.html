Module 48: Non-Standard Nets

Phase 1: The X-Ray

Think of a net as the "Unfolded Cardboard Skeleton" of a 3D shape. While cubes are common, the 11+ often throws weird shapes like pyramids and prisms at your child. The secret weapon is identifying the Base Shape first. Once you know the "Floor," you know exactly how many "Walls" the shape needs to close. Pyramids have one floor and a star of triangles; Prisms have two identical floors connected by a belt of rectangles.

The Pyramid Point "Count the triangles on a square-based pyramid. Why must there be exactly four?"
The Prism Belt "A pentagonal prism is unfolded. How many rectangular panels are needed to wrap it?"
The 11+ Gap Test "Look at an octagonal solid net. How many side-flaps are missing if there are only six rectangles?"

Phase 4: The Saboteur

The "Inventory Only" Trap: Most children assume a net is correct just because it has the right number of shapes. For example, they see a pentagon and 5 triangles and click "Correct." Stop! If those triangles are all clumped on one side of the pentagon, they can't fold up to meet at the top.

The Insider Fix: The "Edge-to-Wall" Rule. Tell your child: "Every side of the floor needs a wall." They should physically count the edges of the base shape. Then, they must check that there is a face ready to fold up from each of those edges. If any side of the floor is "empty," the net is an impostor. Connection beats counting every time.
