Phase 1: Spotting the Pattern

Keeping times matching follows the **Rules of Real Time**. A proper sentence structure requires a single, steady time zone boundary. Most errors happen when an author stops paying attention halfway through a long sentence. We only change time frames when the timeline itself clearly shifts states—like when an ongoing background action gets interrupted by a sudden, quick event (for example: "I was sleeping when the alarm clock rang").

The Unlocked Jump: "She played football and then eats lunch."
An unapproved leap from a Past action word to a Present action word.
The Fact Rule: "The stove is hot and the metal fan blew cold air."
Constant science laws and world facts must always stay anchored in the steady Present tense.
The Interruption Track: "While we were winning the race, a sudden storm stops the match."
The interruption word must match the past time frame of the background scene layer.

Phase 4: Common Mistake to Watch For

The Speech-Bubble Illusion Trap: Children working quickly through errors often try to change the time frame of words trapped inside direct talking speech marks (for example, reading: *The guide stated, "I am lost"* and assuming the word 'am' must be changed to 'was'). This is an error! The speaker inside the quotation marks always talks in their own real-life "now" moment, even if the main story happened a hundred years ago.

The Easy Fix: Isolate the Anchor Verb. Teach your student this simple check: "Find the story's very first action word." Look for the first active action verb that sits completely outside of talking marks. This sets your anchor time stamp. Every regular story action running across that block must match that first stamp. If your anchor word is in the Past, your whole paragraph must stay in the Past!
