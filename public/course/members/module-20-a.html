The Ingredient Swap

Sometimes numbers and letters are "glued" together, like 3x. In Algebra, if they are touching, they are secretly multiplying. Think of it like a recipe: 3x means you need "3 helpings of whatever is in box x."

"We have a formula: 2y + 5. We know that y = 4. Click 'Swap' to pour the 4 into the container. Notice how the 'y' disappears and becomes a '4' inside a protective bracket. Now we can solve it!"

Phase 1: The X-Ray

Think of substitution as a Recipe Swap. You have a formula (the recipe), and you must swap the letters for real numbers. The secret is knowing that touching means multiplying. If the letter is a container, the value is the liquid we pour in.

Simple "If $a=10$ and $b=3$, find $a+b$."
The Glue "If $x=5$ and $y=4$, calculate $3x - 2y$."
Powerplay "If $p=4$ and $q=9$, find $(p^2 + q) \div 5$."

Phase 4: The Saboteur

The "Digit Gluing" Trap: If $A=5$, children see $3A$ and think $35$. They are treating the letter like a digit placeholder!

The Fix: The "Invisible Times" Rule. Algebra is crowded. If they touch, they multiply. $3A$ means $3 \times 5 = 15$. **Always put brackets around your substitution.**
