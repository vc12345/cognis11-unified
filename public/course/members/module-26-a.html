The Magic Rope

Imagine a loop of rope that is 20cm long. You can pull it into a long, skinny rectangle, or push it into a wide square. The rope stays the same length (Perimeter), but the amount of grass it covers (Area) changes as you move it!

"We have a 20cm rope. Click 'Squash' to make it skinny, and 'Stretch' to make it square. Notice that while the rope never gets longer, the green grass inside grows and shrinks. A square always holds the most grass!"

Phase 1: The X-Ray

The perimeter is your "Magic String"—it's the length of the fence. The area is the grass inside. In the 11+, examiners trick you into thinking a fixed perimeter means a fixed area. The opposite is true. By changing the shape, you "leak" or "gain" area. A **Square** is the most efficient shape—it squeezes the most space out of every inch of fence.

The Fence "A garden has a 12m fence. If it is a square, what is the internal area?"
Comparison "Rectangle A is 8x2. Rectangle B is 5x5. Both have a 20cm perimeter. Which has more grass?"
The Wire "A 40cm wire is bent into a 15x5 rectangle, then a 10x10 square. Find the change in area."

Phase 4: The Saboteur

The "Shape-Blind" Trap: Children believe area is a fixed property of perimeter. They think 40cm of string always creates 100cm² of space.

The Fix: The "Square Efficiency" Rule. "Equality is power." The closer the sides are to each other (more square), the larger the area. Long, thin rectangles are space-wasters. Same perimeter does **NOT** mean same area.
