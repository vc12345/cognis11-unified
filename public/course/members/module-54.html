Foundation Intro

The Expression Mask

Every piece of writing has a **Goal** (what it is trying to do) and a **Tone** (how it feels to read). Imagine telling a friend it is raining outside. You could say it as a plain fact, a sarcastic joke, or an angry complaint. The weather is exactly the same, but the "mask" you choose changes the feeling completely!

The Word Sorting Tool

In 11+ exams, you can find the author's tone by looking at their word choices. Action words (**Verbs**) usually show the main purpose or action of the sentence. Describing words (**Adjectives**) reveal the emotional tone, showing exactly how the writer feels about that action.

Phase 1: Spotting the Pattern

Break down reading passsages by looking for the **Author's Real Goal**. Tone tells us the emotional mood of the writing. Purpose tells us the job of the text—why the author wrote it. By separating the **Action Words** from the **Describing Words**, we can easily tell if the author wants to teach us, tease someone, or complain.

The Clean Command: "Clean your bedroom right now!"
Tone: Stern. Main Purpose: To give an order.
The Peaceful Picture: "A golden sun dipped below the soft, quiet clouds."
Tone: Calm. Main Purpose: To describe a scene.
The Sarcastic Joke: "A 'brilliant' computer that works about as well as a paper umbrella."
Tone: Mocking. Main Purpose: To make fun of something.

Phase 2: The Voice Construction Lab

Look at how the sentence parts work together. The mechanical purpose lives inside the **Action Verbs** (the movement), while the emotional tone is built using **Descriptive Adjectives** (the feeling). If we swap the describing words but keep the action words, the basic physical event stays the same, but the mood changes completely.

Phase 4: Common Mistake to Watch For

The "Topic Match" Mistake: Children often confuse the *topic* of a story with its actual *tone*. If a passage describes a massive rainstorm, they might instantly guess the tone is "sad." They are reacting to the weather instead of looking at the exact words the author chose.

The Easy Fix: Do a Describing-Word Audit. Train your child with this step: "Look at the descriptive words, skip the plot summary." Pull out only the adjectives. If a storm is described using words like "standard," "measured," and "recorded," the tone is actually factual and scientific, not sad. True tone lives in the exact vocabulary, never just the topic!
