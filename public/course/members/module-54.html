The Expression Mask

Every piece of writing possesses a specific Author Purpose (the functional job it is trying to achieve) and a unique Emotional Tone (how the prose feels to the reader). Imagine telling a classmate that it has started raining outside. You can deliver that news as a plain statement, a sarcastic joke, or a frustrated complaint. The raw weather event remains identical, but your choice of communication "mask" transforms completely!

Phase 1: Spotting the Pattern

Deconstruct comprehension prompts by tracing out "The Author's True Intent." Tone defines the emotional temperature of the prose—how the creator views the scene. Purpose defines the structural utility of the text segment—why it was put to paper in the first place. By systematically isolating core Action Verbs from flanking Descriptive Adjectives, we reveal if the writer's goal is to assist, mislead, or satirize.

The Authoritative Mandate: "Vacuum your living room immediately!" Tone: Stern. Core Purpose: To Command.
The Scenic Chronicle: "A radiant sun dipped below thick, velvet clouds." Tone: Serene. Core Purpose: To Describe.
The Eleven-Plus Satire: "A technological 'miracle' that proves roughly as useful as a chocolate teapot." Tone: Sarcastic. Core Purpose: To Ridicule.

Phase 4: Common Mistake to Watch For

The "Subject Matter" Association Fallacy: Children frequently mistake the general *topic* of a passage for its inner *tone*. If an exam text focuses entirely on a heavy autumn rainstorm, they often blindly guess that the tone must be "sad." They are emotionally reacting to the raw weather backdrop, completely ignoring the specific words chosen by the writer.

The Easy Fix: Execute a Strict Adjective-Only Audit. Train your child to follow this procedural checklist: "Look directly at the paint color, ignore the plot summary." Copy down only the descriptive modifier words in isolation. If a downpour description relies strictly on words like "standard," "measured," and "noted," the delivery tone is confirmed as Clinical rather than melancholic. True tone thrives inside vocabulary choice, never in the plot outline alone.
