Module 54: Tone & Purpose

Phase 1: The X-Ray

Think of this as "The Author's Intent." In the 11+ Comprehension paper, your child must distinguish between the Voice (Tone) and the Goal (Purpose). Tone is the emotional temperature—how the author feels. Purpose is the mechanical job the text is doing—why it was written. By looking at verbs and adjectives separately, we can figure out if the writer is trying to help us, trick us, or just make us laugh.

The Rule-Maker "Clean your room right now!"
Tone: Stern. Purpose: To Command.
The Story-Teller "The golden sun dipped below the velvet clouds."
Tone: Serene. Purpose: To Describe.
The 11+ Critic "This 'miracle' cure is as useful as a chocolate teapot."
Tone: Sarcastic. Purpose: To Ridicule.

Phase 2: The Anatomy

This is the Semantic Ratio. Help your child see that Purpose is usually hidden in the Verbs (the action parts), while Tone is painted on with Adjectives (the feeling parts). If you change the adjectives but keep the verbs, the purpose stays the same, but the vibe shifts completely.

Phase 4: The Saboteur

The "Topic" Trap: Most children mistake the topic of a story for the tone. For example, if a story is about a rainy day, they assume the tone is "sad." They are reacting to the rain, not the author's actual words.

The Insider Fix: The "Adjective Audit." Tell your child: "Ignore the story, look at the paint." They should list only the descriptive words. If a sad event is described with words like "accurate," "standard," and "noted," the tone is Clinical, not sad. Tone lives in the word choice, not the plot.
