Module 52: Explicit Retrieval

Phase 1: The X-Ray

Think of Explicit Retrieval as "Data Mining." In the Reading Comprehension paper, some answers aren't hidden behind clues—they are literally printed on the page. The secret weapon is the "Hook and Type" system. Your child must learn to ignore the story for a moment and look for a specific Noun (the Hook) and a Question Word (the Type, like "Who" or "When"). Success is about scanning speed, not deep thought.

The Color Catcher "The cat wore a blue collar."
Q: What color was the collar? (Target: Adjective)
The Midnight Runner "Professor Snape left at midnight."
Q: When did the Professor leave? (Target: Time/Date)
The Location Link "The 1892 treaty was signed in Berlin."
Q: Where was the treaty finalized? (Target: Place Name)

Phase 2: The Anatomy

This is the Fact HUD. Before your child looks at the text, they must "program" their brain with a search query. Notice how knowing the "Type" (When) tells the eyes to ignore all the words and search only for numbers or dates. By narrowing the field of vision, they can find the answer in a fraction of the time it takes to re-read the paragraph.

QUERY: "When was the base built?"
TYPE: WHEN (Look for numbers) HOOK: BASE (Target word)
SCAN STATUS: FILTERING FOR TEMPORAL DATA

Phase 4: The Saboteur

The "Enjoyment" Trap: This is the biggest time-waster in the exam. Most children read the entire story from start to finish because they find it interesting. This burns 2-3 minutes of clock time before they even know what the questions are.

The Insider Fix: The "Sacrificial Scan." Tell your child: "Don't read the story, solve the mystery." They should read the question first. Identify the "Hook" (the unique noun) and the "Type" (Who/When/Where). Use a finger to scan the page in a fast 'Z' shape, ignoring every word that doesn't match the target type. Extract the data, then move on.
