Phase 1: Spotting the Pattern

Approach direct comprehension prompts as systematic text "Data Mining." Core solutions aren't hidden behind abstract metaphorical inferences; they are explicitly stamped inside the paragraph block. Your ultimate strategy tool is the "Hook and Target Type" filter process. Program your child's tracking focus with a specific baseline Noun (the tracking hook) and a targeted Question Category (the target type, such as sorting for an active person when meeting "Who" or checking for numerical digits when meeting "When") to completely filter out reading noise.

The Descriptive Modifier: "The pet wore a blue woven leash." Q: What color fill? (Target parameter: Specific Attribute Adjective)
The Temporal Coordinate: "The wizard departed at midnight." Q: What point in time? (Target parameter: Numerical Clock Metric or Date)
The Geographic Anchor: "The treaty was endorsed in Berlin." Q: What regional city? (Target parameter: Capitalized Place Noun)

Phase 4: Common Mistake to Watch For

The Narrative Immersion Time-Sink Trap: Most students reflexively read through the entire story document from top to bottom first simply because they find the plot line engaging. This habit consumes up to three full minutes of precious exam clock time before they have even laid eyes on a single question parameter!

The Easy Fix: Enforce the Tactical Targeted Scan Method. Coach your child with this clear procedural rule: "Skip the recreational reading, isolate the objective variables." Address the worksheet question first. Isolate your unique tracking noun hook and your required data type indicator. Sweep your finger tip down across the page canvas in a high-speed 'Z' trajectory, completely tuning out every surrounding vocabulary unit that does not match your precise target filter. Treat the sheet like an index spreadsheet, not a storybook.
