Module 28: Net Topology & Volume

Phase 1: The X-Ray

Think of this as "Mental Origami." In the 11+, your child will be shown flat paper shapes (nets) and asked to turn them into 3D boxes in their mind. The secret is that boxes have rules about opposites. A Lid and a Base can never touch; in a flat net, they must always be separated by exactly one "buffer" face. If your child can spot these gaps, they can "fold" any box without ever picking up a pair of scissors.

The Cube Core "A cube net has a side of 4cm. What is the total volume when folded?"
The Cuboid Capacity "A net has a 5x3 base and a flap height of 2. How much space is inside the box?"
The Pattern Spotter "Look at this non-standard Z-pattern. Which face will be directly opposite the Base?"

Phase 4: The Saboteur

The "Friendly Neighbor" Trap: Most children assume that if two squares touch (share an edge) on the flat paper, they will be opposite each other in 3D. This is the opposite of the truth! If they touch on paper, they are neighbors in the box.

The Fix: The "Leapfrog" Rule. Tell your child: "Opposites never touch." To find a face's partner, you have to jump over exactly one face. If you pick a square and "leapfrog" over the next one, you’ve found its opposite. If they touch on the net, they are perpendicular, not opposite. One jump = One opposite.
