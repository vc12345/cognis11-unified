Module 24: The Mean Bridge

Phase 1: The X-Ray

Think of the Mean as the "Fair Share" height. In the 11+, examiners love to give you a group of scores and ask: "What must the next person score to change the average?" The secret is ignoring the average for a moment and looking at the Total Pile. To find the missing person, you just compare the pile you have now against the pile you need to hit that target. The "Bridge" is the number that fills the gap.

The Mystery Number "The mean of 3 numbers is 5. Two of the numbers are 4 and 6. What is the 3rd number?"
The Test Scorer "Sarah scored 12, 15, and 10. What must she score next to have a mean of 13?"
The Weight Shift "5 children weigh 40kg on average. A 6th child joins and the mean becomes 41kg. How heavy is the new child?"

Phase 2: The Anatomy

This is the Equality Bridge. On the right, you see the "Ghost Set"—this is what the group would look like if everyone scored exactly the target mean. On the left is your real group. Notice the empty space at the end. When you click "Add Missing Data," the missing score drops in to perfectly level the two sides. The value of that gold bar is the answer to your problem.

Phase 4: The Saboteur

The "Average of Averages" Trap: Most children try to solve this by looking only at the averages. If their current mean is 10 and they want 13, they think: "I just need to add 3 more than the average." Stop! This logic breaks because the group is getting bigger. You aren't just adding 3; you are responsible for lifting the average of the entire new group.

The Fix: The "Total Volume" Rule. Remind your child: "Mean is a mask; Total is the truth." Always find the New Total ($New\ Count \times New\ Mean$) and the Old Total ($Old\ Count \times Old\ Mean$). The difference between those two big piles is always the missing value. Find the big totals first.
