The 100-Square Grid

Every single percentage value is just a simple way of looking at a 100-square grid. When you have 100%, you hold the entire grid in your hands. If someone gives you a 10% bonus, they are simply handing you 10 extra squares. If you lose 20%, those squares just disappear from the grid.

The Shopping Shortcut

In real life, we want to find the final checkout price instantly. Instead of finding 10% of an item first and then laboriously taking it away, we use a single Shortcut Number. If a toy is 20% off, it means you are still choosing to pay for 80% of it. We just multiply the original tag price by 0.8 to reveal the final cost in one swift move!

"Imagine we are at the shops. This board game costs £50, but it has a 20% discount. Instead of using two separate math steps, let's use our shortcut number: 0.8. Notice how the price tag updates instantly? That is because you are only paying for 80% of the game's original value."

Phase 1: Spotting the Pattern

In 11+ exams, long multi-step percentage questions are a classic Speed Trap. Finding 10%, then doubling it, then adding it onto the original takes far too much valuable time. We teach children to use the **One-Step Shortcut**. By treating the starting item as a baseline of 1.00, we can multiply just once to find the final answer. This turns a wordy problem into a quick 5-second calculation.

The High Street Sale: "A coat costing £20 is reduced by 15% in a sale. Find its new final price tag."
The Bonus Pack: "A box of 40 marbles arrives with a 20% 'extra free' bonus inside. What is the new total number of marbles?"
The Price Rise: "A train ticket costing £40 increases in value by exactly 12%. What is the new cost of the ticket?"

Phase 4: Common Mistake to Watch For

The "Simple Addition" Trap: If an item costs £50 and increases by 10%, many children make the mistake of simply adding the number 10 onto the price (giving an answer of £60). They are treating a percentage like a normal £10 note.

The Easy Fix: The "Shortcut Multiplier." Remind your child directly: "Percentages are built for multiplying, not adding." Turn that 10% jump into its single shortcut format (1.1) and multiply: 50 × 1.1 = £55. This keeps the extra portion perfectly scaled to the item's size.
