Foundation Intro

The Pizza Party

Fractions are just instructions for sharing a pizza. The Bottom Number (the Knife) tells you how many equal slices to cut. The Top Number (the Hand) tells you how many slices to put on your plate.

"We have one pizza. If we want to find 3/4, we first use the 'Bottom Knife' to cut it into 4 equal pieces. Then, we use our 'Top Hand' to take 3 of them. Watch how the fraction describes the meal!"

The Treasure Chests

When sharing money, we don't cut it with a knife. We divide it into Equal Chests. To find 2/5 of £100, we first divide the whole £100 into 5 chests. Once we know what 1 chest holds, we just "collect" the 2 that we need.

"We have £100. The fraction is 2/5, so we need 5 chests. Look inside just one chest—that's £20! Since the top number is 2, we pick up two chests to get our final total of £40."

Phase 1: The X-Ray

Think of a fraction as two simple instructions: Slice and Collect. The bottom number tells you how many equal slices to cut your total into. The top number tells you how many of those slices to put on your plate. To solve any 11+ fraction problem, we always find the "weight" of just one slice first.

Level 1 "Find 1/2 of 12 sweets. How many in each of the two groups?"
Level 2 "A cake weighs 500g and is cut into 5 slices. If 3 are eaten, what is the mass?"
Level 3 "A 40km race is 5/8 complete. How many km are left?"

Phase 4: The Saboteur

The "Upside-Down" Mistake: Children often try to divide by the top number because it’s the first one they see. This leads to massive, confusing results.

The Fix: The "Knife and Hand" Rule. The bottom number is the Knife (divide). The top number is the Hand (multiply). Always find one piece first. If the answer looks bigger than the starting total, the fraction has gone upside down!
