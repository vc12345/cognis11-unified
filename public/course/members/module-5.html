Foundation Intro

The Sticker Machine

This method is a simple "Copycat Rule." If the number of items you buy doubles, the number of coins you must pay doubles too. Both groups are locked together. If one side grows, the other side matches it perfectly.

"We want to buy some reward stickers. Each sticker costs exactly 2 gold coins. Watch what happens to our stack of coins as we change the slider to add more stickers. They always grow at the exact same speed!"

The Baking Trick

To scale a recipe up for a larger group of family members, we first look at the Single Share (the exact amount of ingredients 1 person gets). Once we hold that key amount, we can easily multiply it to feed a dinner party of 10 people or an entire school!

Phase 1: Spotting the Pattern

Matching rules rely on an invisible link: if you buy double the items, you pay double the cost. The master shortcut to solving these questions in exams is always uncovering the Price for Just One first. Once your child tracks down that single unit value, they can scale it up to calculate any total size with total confidence.

The Sticker Shop: "If 2 identical sticker packs cost 10p, how much do 6 sticker packs cost?"
The Kitchen Scale: "A sponge cake recipe for 4 people uses 300g of sugar. How many grams are required to bake for 10 people?"
The Road Map: "On a map scale where every single centimeter represents a fixed real distance, a 4cm line means 12 miles. How many miles does an 8cm path show?"

Phase 3: Interactive Practice

Move the sliders together. Notice how finding the Share of One is the key step. Your child will see that the amount needed per single person never changes, no matter how large the group gets.

Phase 4: Common Mistake to Watch For

The "Just Add a Bit" Mistake: If 4 people need 100g of flour, and the question scales up to 6 people, children often think: "That is 2 more people, so I will just add 2 more grams of flour." They are treating a balanced mixture like a pile of blocks.

The Easy Fix: The "Taste Test." Ask them directly: "If we double the people but only add a tiny pinch of ingredients, will our cake still taste right?" Remind them that proportion is a **Multiplication Game**. If the crowd size multiplies, your ingredients must multiply by the exact same setting to keep the taste perfect.
