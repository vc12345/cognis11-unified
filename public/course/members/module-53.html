Foundation Intro

The Detective's Clue Bag

Finding hidden clues means acting exactly like a real detective. The author might not tell you directly where a scene takes place, but they will leave clues behind. For example, if a story mentions muddy footprints tracking across a doormat, you know for sure that someone just walked inside. We call this making an inference—using clues to discover the unwritten truth.

The Reading Secret Equation

To solve tricky reading questions, coach your child to use this simple formula: Take the Text Evidence (the exact words written on the page) and add your own Real-World Knowledge (what you already know about life). Putting them together unlocks the hidden meaning.

"Let's look at this sentence: 'The wind howled through the leafless trees.' The page tells us there are no leaves on the branches. Our real-world knowledge tells us that trees drop their leaves when it gets cold. Click 'Blend Evidence and Logic' to watch the hidden truth appear!"
Leafless Trees + What I Know About Life = It is Winter
Text Evidence + Real Life Knowledge = Hidden Truth.

Phase 1: Spotting the Pattern

Approach advanced reading questions by teaching your child to hunt for "The Double Clue Lock." A safe guess is never based on just one lonely word. You want to match a text clue directly with what you know about life. If you can find two independent clues pointing to the same answer, you have locked in a successful deduction!

Weather Clues: "Leo grabbed his umbrella and zipped his rubber boots up tight." (Two clues match life knowledge = It is raining).
Time Clues: "Long shadows stretched across the grass as the street lamps flickered on." (Two clues match life knowledge = It is late evening).
Feeling Clues: "Sarah's handwriting looked shaky on the note, and her fingers began to tremble." (Two clues match life knowledge = She is terrified).

Phase 4: Common Mistake to Watch For

The "Wild Guess" Illusion Trap: Children racing through comprehension tests often make up wild stories that aren't supported by the page (like reading 'He picked up a bag' and deciding 'He must be flying to America!'). This is an imaginary guess, not a real conclusion.

The Easy Fix: Ask the "Prove It" Question. Train your child to act like a lawyer: "Show me the proof." For every answer they circle, they must be able to put their finger on two separate words in the text that support it. If they can't find a double-clue anchor, tell them to drop that answer immediately.
