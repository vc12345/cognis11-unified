Module 53: Implicit Inference

Phase 1: The X-Ray

Think of inference as "Reading the Shadows." In the 11+, examiners won't always tell you how a character feels or what the weather is like. Instead, they give you clues. The secret weapon is realizing that an inference is a bridge. It requires your child to take a piece of Evidence from the text and glue it to their own Real-World Logic. If they can’t prove it using words from the page, it’s just a guess—not an inference.

Evidence (Page) + Logic (Brain) = Inference
The Weather Detective "Leo grabbed his umbrella and yellow boots."
Q: What is the weather? (A: It is raining).
The Time Tracker "Shadows lengthened and the street lamps flickered on."
Q: What time is it? (A: Evening/Dusk).
The Emotion Expert "Arthur’s jagged scrawl was smeared by his trembling hand."
Q: How does Arthur feel? (A: Terrified or Anxious).

Phase 2: The Anatomy

This is the Double Anchor. A strong inference is never based on just one word. Look at the sentence below. We know the man is angry not just because his face is red, but because he slammed the phone. Tell your child: "One clue is a guess; two clues is a lock." They should always hunt for that second "Anchor" to be 100% sure of their answer.

"The red-faced man slammed the phone down."
Conclusion: The man is furious.

Phase 4: The Saboteur

The "Movie Script" Trap: Most children treat the text like a movie and add details that simply aren't there. For example, if a character is "carrying a bag," they assume the character is "going to the airport" because that's what happens in films. This is a Projection, not an Inference. It’s a guess, and it will lose them points.

The Insider Fix: The "Prove It" Test. Tell your child: "Be a lawyer." For every answer they choose, they must be able to point to two separate words in the text that support it. If they can only find one (or zero!), their answer is a guess. No evidence = No points.
