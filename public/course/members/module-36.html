Module 36: Anagram Scrambler

Phase 1: The X-Ray

Think of an anagram as a "Re-boxed Recipe." In the 11+, examiners love to hide synonyms or antonyms by scrambling their letters. The secret weapon is realizing that an anagram is just a different arrangement of the exact same "stock." To find the truth, your child must look past the sound of the word and perform a Character Inventory. Two words are only valid anagrams if their "Digital Fingerprint"—their list of letters—is identical.

The Direct Check "Are 'ACT' and 'CAT' anagrams? Count the ingredients in both to find out."
The Scrambled Synonym "Rearrange the letters in 'LISTEN' to find a synonym for 'QUIET'."
The 11+ Impostor "Identify the outlier that is NOT an anagram of the others: 'RETAIN', 'REINA', 'TERNIA'."

Phase 4: The Saboteur

The "One Letter Off" Trap: Most children see DESSERT and DESERT and assume they are anagrams because they "look" identical. The examiner is banking on their brain being lazy. They see the same types of letters and stop counting.

The Insider Fix: The "Alphabetical Stock-Take." Tell your child: "Don't trust your eyes; trust the count." Have them mentally (or physically) sort the letters into A-Z order. Word A: [D-E-S-S-E-R-T] becomes [D-E-E-R-S-S-T]. Word B: [D-E-S-E-R-T] becomes [D-E-E-R-S-T]. The mismatch jumps out instantly. If the counts don't match, the bridge is broken.
