Module 28: Net Topology & Volume

Foundation Intro

The Folding Box

A Net is just a box that has been unfolded and flattened out. When we fold it back up, the squares that used to be far apart might end up becoming neighbors, while neighbors might stay close together.

"Look at this flat pattern of 6 squares. This is the net of a cube. Click 'Fold' to see how these flat shapes rise up to form a solid box. Notice that the Base and the Lid are never next to each other on the paper!"

Foundation Progressing

The Leapfrog Rule

In the 11+, you don't have time to fold boxes in your head. Instead, use the Leapfrog Rule. On a flat net, opposite faces (like Top and Bottom) are always separated by exactly one square. If you can jump over a neighbor, you’ve found the opposite!

"We want to find the face opposite the Gold one. We don't need to imagine it folding. Just jump over the next square in a straight line. Click the square you think is the opposite!"
Find the partner for the Gold face. Jump over one!

Phase 1: The X-Ray

Think of this as "Mental Origami." Boxes have strict rules about opposites. A Lid and a Base can never touch; in a flat net, they must always be separated by exactly one "buffer" face. If you spot these gaps, you can "fold" any box instantly. The internal space is simply the Base Area times the Height.

Phase 4: The Saboteur

The "Friendly Neighbor" Trap: Children assume sharing an edge on flat paper means they are opposite in 3D. **Wrong!** If they touch on paper, they are neighbors in the box.

The Fix: The "Leapfrog" Rule. "Opposites never touch." To find a partner, you must jump over exactly one face. **One jump = One opposite.**
