Module 27: The Turning Corner Rules

Foundation Intro

The Scissor Twins

Whenever two straight straight paths cross over each other, they generate a clean mirror image. If the top opening of a pair of scissors stretches wider, the bottom opening must automatically copy it and widen by the exact same amount. We describe these as Perfect Mirror Twins, and their corner values are always identical.

"Take a look at the interactive scissor blades below. Drag the slider handle to open and shut them. Notice how the bottom corner mimics the top corner with absolute perfection. They are locked together. If the top corner opens to 180°, its mirror twin directly across the pivot point must balance at 180° too!"

The Rubber Ink Stamp

If a slanted line cuts clean through two completely straight Parallel Tracks (just like train rails), it builds two identical matching intersections. You can visualize the top junction as a rubber ink stamp that gets pressed directly down onto the lower track line. The turning values do not morph or alter—they simply repeat in a new spot!

"We are tracking a 60° opening on our upper track line. Because these railway lines run perfectly parallel, we can copy-paste that exact corner measurement down to the lower path. Click the 'Stamp the Pattern!' button to watch the value slide down into place. We call these Matched Landing Spots."

Phase 1: Spotting the Pattern

Think of corner tracking on matching paths as "The Shadow Silhouette Rule." In competitive 11+ geometry problems, you only ever need to juggle two core numbers. Every single corner in a parallel track map belongs to one of two family branches. They are either identical twins matching each other's sizes, or they combine together to equal exactly 180° to create a perfect flat line.

The Flat Road Line: "All opening corners resting along a flat, straight road segment must combine to make 180°. If one side measures 110°, what value belongs to its direct neighbor?"
The Zig-Zag Alternate Switch: "A slanted boundary cuts across two tracks. If the top-right corner locks at 60°, what is the value of its 'Alternate Zig-Zag' partner down below?"
The Interior C-Squeeze: "If one interior corner trapped inside an overlapping C-shape measures 75°, why must its interior partner scale exactly to 105°?"

Phase 4: Common Mistake to Watch For

The "Looks Straight Enough" Illusion Trap: Children often spot two layout lines that look roughly like train tracks and immediately start calculating using alternate zig-zag rules. This is a dangerous exam speed trap! If the problem lines are not perfectly parallel, the top intersection has no mathematical relationship to the lower intersection.

The Easy Fix: The "Arrowhead Hunt." Train your student: "The calculation rules are completely locked out unless the layout arrows appear." Always search the problem sketch for matched arrow markings or explicit text stating "Parallel Lines." No matching arrows means you cannot jump values between lines.
