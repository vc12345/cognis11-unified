The Decimal Trap

The single biggest point-stealer in 11+ time word problems is writing out a duration like 1.5 hours. Our brains naturally want to scan that value and read it as "1 hour and 50 minutes." But look at our interactive clock! That .5 extension simply represents a half-full container. In a clock container that spans 60 full minutes, half capacity is exactly 30 minutes, not 50.

"Drag the slider control over to the 0.5 marker. Look at the gold hand tracking across the clock face. It is pointing directly downward at the 30-minute line. Writing down 0.5 is simply a mathematical shortcut for saying 'Half an Hour'. Do not let the decimal point trick you into calculating out of 100!"

Phase 1: Spotting the Pattern

In competitive entrance papers, clocks are where normal decimal math stops working. We must calculate using the 60-Minute Rule. The core trick is managing what examiners call the "Overflow Threshold." As soon as any collection of minutes climbs to 60, it must be bundled up into a single hour block. True exam speed comes from knowing how to safely translate totals back and forth across these 60-minute boundaries.

The Baking Timer: "A gourmet cake needs to bake inside the oven for 85 minutes. How many full hours and leftover minutes is that?"
The Travel Route: "An international train trip takes exactly 1 hour and 45 minutes to arrive. Find the total duration written entirely in minutes."
The Endurance Track: "A marathon runner crosses the stadium finish line after 215 minutes of running. Translate that total duration into Hours and Minutes."

Phase 4: Common Mistake to Watch For

Phase 4: Common Mistake to Watch For

The "Decimal Illusion" Trap: Rushed students read a speed question mentioning 1.5 hours and instantly jump to the conclusion that it means 1 hour and 50 minutes. They are accidentally forcing base-10 rules onto a clock face!

The Easy Fix: The "Half-Tank" Rule. Time tracking does not follow metric baselines. Because our standard container width is locked at 60 minutes, a 0.5 decimal share (exactly one half) will always equal 30 minutes. If your child defaults to using 100 as the base, the exam trap wins the points.
