Module 6: Percentage Multipliers

Foundation Intro

The 100-Square Map

Every percentage is just a way of describing a 100-square grid. If you have "100%," you have the whole grid. If someone gives you a 10% bonus, they just hand you 10 extra squares. If you lose 20%, those squares simply vanish.

"Look at the grid. This is our 100%. If we add 20%, watch the gold squares appear. We now have 120 squares in total. It’s much easier to see it as one big group than as two separate math problems, isn't it?"

The Discount Gun

In the real world, we want the final price fast. Instead of finding 10% and then subtracting it, we use a Shortcut Number. If an item is 20% off, it means you are still paying for 80% of it. We just multiply the price by 0.8 and—zap—the new tag is ready!

"We’re at the shops. This toy costs £50, but there's a 20% discount. Instead of doing two sums, let's use the shortcut 0.8. See how the price tag changes instantly? You're paying for 80% of the toy."

Phase 1: The X-Ray

In the 11+, multi-step percentage problems are a Speed Trap. Finding 10%, then doubling it, then adding it takes too long. We use the One-Step Shortcut. By treating the original as 1.00, we multiply once to get the final answer. This turns a word problem into a 5-second calculation.

The Sale "A £20 item is 15% off. Find the new price."
The Bonus "40 marbles get a 20% 'extra free' bonus. What is the new total?"
The Hike "A £40 item increases by 12%. What is the new cost?"

Phase 3: The Engine

Toggle between increase and decrease. Watch the Shortcut Number. Notice how a 20% increase is x1.2, while a 20% decrease is x0.8.

Phase 4: The Saboteur

The "Simple Addition" Trap: If a price is £50 and goes up 10%, many kids just add the number 10 (making £60). They treat the percentage like a £10 note.

The Fix: The "Shortcut Multiplier." Remind them: "Percentages are for multiplying." Turn the 10% into its shortcut (1.1) and multiply: 50 × 1.1 = £55. This keeps the "extra" proportionate.
