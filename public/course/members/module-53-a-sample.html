The Inference Equation

To win at the 11+, you must follow a formula. You take the **Evidence** (what the book says) and add your **Logic** (what you already know). The answer is the **Inference**.

"Read this: 'The wind howled through the leafless trees.' Evidence: No leaves. Logic: Trees lose leaves in winter. Click 'Solve' to see the inference. Remember: if you can't point to evidence, you're just guessing!"
LEAFLESS TREES + MY LOGIC = IT IS WINTER

Preview Stage 2: Supplemental Mastery (Years 4–6)

Phase 1: The X-Ray

Think of inference as "Reading the Shadows." Success relies on the Double Anchor rule. A strong inference is never based on one word. You must glue textual Evidence to Real-World Logic.

Weather "Leo grabbed his umbrella and yellow boots." Q: What's the weather? (Rain).
Time "Shadows lengthened and lamps flickered on." Q: What time? (Evening).
Emotion "Arthur’s jagged scrawl was smeared by his trembling hand." Q: Feeling? (Terrified).

Phase 2: The Anatomy

The Double Anchor. Look at the sentence below. We know the man is furious because of two anchors: his "red face" AND the fact he "slammed" the phone. "One clue is a guess; two clues is a lock."

"The red-faced man slammed the phone down."
Conclusion: The man is furious.

Phase 3: The Engine

Use the Evidence Well. Click the underlined phrases to collect them. Once you have a Double Anchor (2 clues), test the logic against the conclusions.

"Sarah's stomach somersaulted as she stood behind the heavy velvet curtain. She gripped her violin bow so tightly her knuckles turned white."
Click clues to find the Double Anchor...

Phase 4: The Saboteur

The "Movie Script" Trap: Children add details not in the text. (e.g. "He has a bag, so he's going to the airport"). This is a Projection, not an Inference.

The Fix: The "Prove It" Test. For every answer, you must point to two separate words in the text that support it. **No evidence = No points.**
