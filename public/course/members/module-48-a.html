Module 48: Non-Standard Nets

Foundation Intro

The Flower Petals

A Pyramid net is like a flower. The center is the **Base** (the floor), and the triangles are the **Petals**. To close the pyramid, every petal must fold up to meet at a single point in the middle.

"Look at our square floor. It has 4 sides. That means it needs exactly 4 petals to cover it. Click 'Fold Petal' to see them prepare to meet. If a petal was missing, the flower couldn't close!"
The square floor needs 4 triangle walls.
Foundation Progressing

The Prism Sandwich

A Prism is different from a pyramid. It has **two identical floors** (like slices of bread) connected by a belt of rectangles (the filling). The number of rectangles must match the number of sides on the floor.

"Look at this net. We have two triangles. They are the 'bread' of our sandwich. Since a triangle has 3 sides, we need exactly 3 rectangles to wrap around them. Let's count the rectangles together."
Triangular Prism: 2 Bases + 3 Walls
Check the floor: 3 sides = 3 rectangles.

Phase 1: The X-Ray

Think of a net as the "Unfolded Cardboard Skeleton." Success starts with identifying the Base Shape. Once you know the "Floor," you know exactly how many "Walls" the shape needs to close. Pyramids have one floor and a star of triangles; Prisms have two identical floors connected by a belt of rectangles.

Pyramid Point "Count the triangles on a square-based pyramid. Why exactly four?"
Prism Belt "A pentagonal prism is unfolded. How many rectangular panels wrap it?"
Gap Test "An octagonal solid net has only six rectangles. How many are missing?"

Phase 2: The Anatomy

The Blueprint Generator. Notice that as the Floor gains more sides, the Walls must increase to match. If the floor has 5 sides but the net only has 4 walls, the shape has a "leak."

Phase 3: The Engine

Toggle between geometry types. Observe the Trace Log confirm the mathematical relationship between the base sides and the resulting faces.

Phase 4: The Saboteur

The "Inventory Only" Trap: Most children assume a net is correct if it has the right number of shapes. **False.** If 5 triangles clump on one side of a pentagon, they cannot fold to meet at the top.

The Fix: The "Edge-to-Wall" Rule. "Every floor side needs a wall." Count the base edges. Ensure a face folds up from **each** edge. If an edge is empty, the net is an impostor.
