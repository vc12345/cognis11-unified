Module 26: Area/Perimeter Invariance

Phase 1: The X-Ray

Think of this as "The Magic String." The perimeter is the length of your string (the fence), and the area is the grass inside. In the 11+, examiners love to trick children into thinking that if the fence stays the same length, the garden stays the same size. Wrong. By simply changing the shape, you can "leak" or "gain" area. The secret is that a Square is the most efficient shape—it squeezes the most space out of every inch of fence.

The Garden Fence "A garden has a 12m fence. If it's a square, what is the internal area?"
The Comparison "Rectangle A is 8cm x 2cm. Rectangle B is 5cm x 5cm. Both have a 20cm perimeter, but which has more grass?"
The Wire Challenge "A 40cm wire is bent into a 15x5 rectangle, then a 10x10 square. Which shape creates more space?"

Phase 4: The Saboteur

The "Shape-Blind" Trap: Many children think area is a fixed property of perimeter. They believe that if you have 40cm of string, you always get 100cm² of space, no matter how you lay it out on the table.

The Fix: The "Square Efficiency" Rule. Tell your child: "Equality is power." The closer the side lengths are to each other (the more "square" the shape), the larger the area will be. A long, thin rectangle is a space-waster. A balanced square is a space-maker. Same perimeter does NOT mean same area.
