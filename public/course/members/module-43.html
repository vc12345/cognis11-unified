The Distance Twins Rule

Every single point on a shape possesses an identical "Distance Twin" directly across the line of symmetry. If a corner point sits exactly 3 steps away from the mirror line on the left, its matching twin must measure exactly 3 steps away on the right.

Phase 1: Spotting the Pattern

Think of mirror symmetry problems as avoiding "The Mirror Illusion Trap." On complex 11+ non-verbal tests, exam designers use flipped orientations to camouflage simple patterns. Your student's primary defense weapon is the Equidistant Rule: every feature maintains an identical distance partner. If the "arms" of a shape point outwards away from the red axis, the reflected arms must point outwards too. Mirrors reverse directions, but they preserve spatial measurements perfectly.

The Vertical Fold Line: "Reflect a square block over a vertical line. Does the overall form change shape positionally?"
Asymmetry Balance: "Apply a horizontal flip to a capital letter 'P'. Which way does the rounded loop face post-transformation?"
The Eleven-Plus Diagonal: "Execute a reflection across a tilted 45-degree line. Where does the top corner block land?"

Phase 4: Common Mistake to Watch For

The "Sliding Shift" Illusion Trap: Many children treat a reflection problem like a simple slide (translation). They shift the shape over the line without turning its face around. For example, if the letter 'F' faces right on the source side, they keep it facing right on the answer side.

The Easy Fix: The Outer Corner Count Method. Train your child with this catchphrase: "The part furthest from the wall stays furthest from the wall." If a specific tail tip is the most distant feature on the left, it must remain the most distant feature on the right. Count grid boxes to confirm the flip.
