Module 43: Reflection Logic

Phase 1: The X-Ray

Think of reflection as "The Mirror Trap." In the 11+, examiners love to confuse your child by showing shapes that have been flipped upside down or side-to-side. The secret weapon is the Equidistant Rule: every point on the original shape has a twin on the other side of the mirror. If a corner is 2 inches away from the mirror line on the left, its twin must be exactly 2 inches away on the right. If the "arms" of a shape point away from the mirror, the reflected arms must point away too.

The Vertical Flip "Reflect a simple square across a vertical line. Does the shape change?"
The Asymmetry Test "Perform a horizontal flip on a 'P' shape. Which way is the loop facing now?"
The 11+ Diagonal "Identify the result of a reflection across a 45° diagonal line. Where does the top corner land?"

Phase 2: The Anatomy

This is the Mirror Axis. Point to the red dashed line—that is your mirror. Pick one corner of the gold 'F' shape. Count how many grid squares it is from the mirror. In a true reflection, that corner must land the exact same distance away on the other side. Use the Equidistant Rule to track the furthest points first; they are the easiest to spot in a lineup.

Phase 4: The Saboteur

The "Slide" Trap: Many children think reflection is just a simple slide (translation). They assume the shape just moves to the other side of the line without changing its "face." If the 'F' is facing right on the left side, they'll draw it facing right on the right side too.

The Insider Fix: The "Corner Count." Tell your child: "The furthest point stays the furthest." Pick the tip of the shape. If it's the furthest thing on the left, it must become the furthest thing on the right. If they just slide the shape, that tip will suddenly be the closest thing to the mirror. Count the gaps to prove the flip.
