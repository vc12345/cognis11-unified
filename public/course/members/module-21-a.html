Module 21: Venn Diagrams (2 Circles)

Foundation Intro

The Land of Both

Venn Diagrams are just sorting baskets with a twist. Some things are Red, some things are Round. But what if something is Red AND Round? It can't be in two places at once, so we create a shared middle basket called the Both zone.

"We have a bowl of fruit. Some are Green (Apples), some are Small (Grapes). But look at the Green Grape—it satisfies both rules! Click 'Sort Fruit' to see how the Green Grape finds its home in the middle."

Foundation Progressing

The Room Share

In the 11+, groups always overlap. If Group A has 10 people and 3 are also in Group B, then Group A only has **7 people who are ONLY in A**. We must subtract the shared middle to see the true numbers.

"Imagine two rooms. Room A has 15 children. But 5 of them are actually sitting in the doorway sharing with Room B. If we don't subtract those 5, we count them twice and the total is wrong! Look how the 'Only' numbers change when they slide together."
Room A: 15 | Room B: 10.

Phase 1: The X-Ray

Think of a Venn Diagram as "The Land of Both." The secret weapon is the Intersection ($A \cap B$)—the sweet spot where things satisfy both rules. Adding the two group totals together creates a "double count" trap. To find the truth, always subtract the overlap from the totals to see who is "Only" in a group.

The Sports Club "12 footballers, 3 also play rugby. How many play football ONLY?"
The Merge "Group A has 20, Group B has 15. 5 are in both. Total unique people?"
The Minimum "40 students: 25 like Art, 20 like Music. Smallest number who must like both?"

Phase 2: The Anatomy

This is the Overlap Shift. Notice how the numbers in the main circles shrink as soon as the Intersection (the red number) appears. We haven't lost any people; we've just moved them into the "Both" room to avoid double-counting.

Phase 3: The Engine

Adjust the population sizes. Watch the Trace Log to see the "Logic Subtraction" that ensures every person is only counted once.

Phase 4: The Saboteur

The "Blind Addition" Trap: Most children see "15 Footballers" and "10 Swimmers" and say "25 total." They've accidentally invented extra people if 5 do both!

The Fix: The "Once Only" Rule. Subtract the intersection from both sides first. **Clean the groups, then add them up.** $Total = (A-Int) + (B-Int) + Int$.
