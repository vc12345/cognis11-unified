Phase 1: Spotting the Pattern

Think of a geometric pattern grid as managing "The Logical Intersection Rule." Every individual cell box is governed simultaneously by two separate design laws: the horizontal Row Rule (controlling how details change sideways or spin) and the vertical Column Rule (controlling properties up and down like color fill). The missing element is the flawless blend of these two matching vectors. Because we use asymmetric shapes, rotations can be easily tracked at a single glance!

The Shape Mapping Track: "Row 1: Red L-Shape, Blue L-Shape, Green L-Shape. Row 2: Red Flag, Blue Flag, [?]. (Deduce the missing color and item block)."
The Rotational Shift Grid: "Asymmetric icons spin exactly 90 degrees clockwise inside every successive cell block across the row, making orientation shifts distinct."
The Eleven-Plus Dual Fusion: "Overall shape orientation rotates down columns, while color shading alternates across structural rows."

Phase 4: Common Mistake to Watch For

The "Single-Track Focus" Speed Trap: Children racing against the clock frequently identify one basic pattern rule (such as "this bottom row must contain pointers") and instantly circle the first option they spot on their answer paper—completely ignoring changes in color or rotation angle.

The Easy Fix: Apply the Isolated Cross-Hair Method. Train your student with this shortcut: "Isolate the evidence details." Filter out Attribute 1 (Base Shape outline) by scanning the rows alone. Next, isolate Attribute 2 (Color Fill or Angular Tilt) by evaluating the column paths. The correct answer must be the exact sum of both distinct facts.
