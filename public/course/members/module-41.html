Phase 1: Spotting the Pattern

Think of sequential layout questions as applying a strict "Detective's Filter." Gaining the upper hand does not depend on guessing the final arrangement right away—it relies on eliminating the impossible combinations. Treat every clue like a fine mesh screen that catches and blocks out faulty options. By the time you filter your list through every statement, only one undeniable truth remains standing.

The Simple Pair: "A is taller than B. If there are only two friends in total, who must be the shortest?"
The Trio Chain: "B is taller than A, and C is taller than B. Arrange their positions from shortest to tallest."
The Eleven-Plus Master: "D is verified as tallest. B is taller than A but shorter than C. Calculate the exact sequence."

Phase 4: Common Mistake to Watch For

The "Immediate Placement" Shortcut Trap: Children under pressure often spot a clue like "A is taller than B" and instantly assign A to the absolute first slot on their sheet. This is an exam trap! Knowing A > B simply means A sits ahead of B; it does not prove that A is the tallest overall choice in the group.

The Easy Fix: Establish the Clue Chain First. Teach your student this golden rule: "Never build the wall until you have linked the bricks together." Force them to connect independent clues together on scrap paper first (combining A > B and B > C into a clear, unified A > B > C chain). Always hook your clues together before labeling final rank positions.
