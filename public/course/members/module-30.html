Module 30: Relative Letter Shift

Phase 1: The X-Ray

Think of this as "The Secret Service Code." In the 11+, your child will encounter words hidden behind a "shift"—where each letter has been moved a certain number of steps down the alphabet. The secret weapon is Pattern Persistence. Once your child figures out the "Key" (how many steps 'A' moved to become its new letter), they must apply that exact same jump to every other letter in the word to crack the code.

The Single Step "If 'CAT' is coded as 'DBU', what is the shift? Use that same pattern to code 'DOG'."
The Decoder Ring "In a secret code, 'FISH' becomes 'HKUJ'. Find the key and decode the mystery word 'BIRD'."
The Reverse Riddle "The code is flipped (A=Z, B=Y). If the message is 'BCZ', what was the original word?"

Phase 2: The Anatomy

This is the Concentric Cipher. The outer ring is the "Normal" alphabet; the inner ring is the "Secret" alphabet. Point to the gold line at the top—that’s the Sync Point. When you rotate the inner ring, the relationship between every letter pair shifts. Help your child notice that the distance between letters never changes; the whole alphabet moves together like a set of locked gears.

Phase 3: The Engine

Use the slider to rotate the inner dial and type a word into the box. Watch the Trace Log as it translates each letter using your custom key. Ask your child to predict the coded letter for 'A' before they move the slider—if they can guess one, they can solve the whole word.

Phase 4: The Saboteur

The "Alphabet Wall" Trap: Most children visualize the alphabet as a flat line. When a shift moves past 'Z', they freeze because they think the alphabet has "run out" of room. If the code is $+2$ and they need to code 'Y', they get stuck.

The Insider Fix: The "Alphabet Clock" Rule. Tell your child: "The alphabet is a ring, not a ruler." Just like a clock goes from 12 back to 1, the alphabet goes from Z back to A. If they hit the 'Wall' at Z, they just keep counting from the start. Z is not the end; it's just the bridge back to A.
