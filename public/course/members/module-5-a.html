Module 5: Direct Proportion

Foundation Intro

The Sticker Machine

Direct Proportion is a "Copycat" Rule. If the number of stickers doubles, the number of coins we need must double too. They are locked together. If one goes up, the other follows in perfect lockstep.

"We want to buy some stickers. Each sticker costs exactly 2 coins. Watch what happens to our 'Coin Pile' as we add more stickers to the machine. They grow together, don't they?"

The Recipe Scaler

To cook for a bigger group, we find the "Unit Share" (how much 1 person gets). Once we know that, we can multiply it to feed 10 people or 100 people! It's like having a magic zoom button for your kitchen.

"This bowl holds a recipe for 2 people. If we want to cook for 8 people, the bowl needs to be 4 times fuller. Watch the ingredients grow as we change the 'Target Group'."

Phase 1: The X-Ray

Direct Proportion is "Perfect Lockstep." If you buy twice as many stickers, you pay twice as much. The secret weapon is finding the Value of One first. If your child can find the "unit price," they can scale up to any number with total confidence.

The Sticker Shop "If 2 stickers cost 10p, how much do 6 stickers cost?"
The Kitchen Scale "A recipe for 4 people uses 300g of sugar. How much for 10 people?"
The Map "On a 1:25,000 scale map, an 8cm road represents a massive real distance. Find it."

Phase 2: The Anatomy

Think of this as a Photocopier. The "Multiplier" is the zoom setting. Watch the Output grow. It doesn't just get bigger; it follows the exact same "times table" rule every single time. Direct proportion never adds; it only multiplies.

Phase 3: The Engine

Adjust the quantities and watch the Value of One. Help your child see that the "Weight per Person" stays exactly the same, no matter the group size.

Phase 4: The Saboteur

The "Just Add a Bit" Trap: If 4 people need 10g of salt, and we scale to 6, children often think: "That's 2 more people, so I'll add 2g salt." They are treating a recipe like a pile of blocks.

The Fix: The "Taste Test." Ask: "If we only add a tiny bit, will it still taste the same?" Proportion is a Multiplication Game. If the quantity doubles, the ingredient MUST double to keep the taste perfect.
