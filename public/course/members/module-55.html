Module 55: Provable Facts vs. Personal Opinions

Foundation Intro

The Ruler vs. The Heart Test

A true Provable Fact is information that you can measure or verify using a tool—like a real ruler, a calendar, or a clock. A Personal Opinion shows how someone feels inside their own heart. Describing a feeling doesn't mean the person is wrong, it just means we cannot use a tool to measure it as mathematical data!

"Look at this sentence: 'The cake is 10cm tall.' Can we use a real ruler to prove that? Yes, we can! That makes it a solid fact. But if we change it to 'The cake is delicious,' can a ruler prove that? No! 'Delicious' is a feeling in your heart, which makes it an opinion. Click the tools below to check the sentence!"

Phase 1: Spotting the Pattern

Approach advanced reading passages by using a strict **Word Filter**. In reading exercises, your child must completely separate verifiable **Facts** (things we can check with tools or records) from **Opinions** (judgments based on personal taste). To score high marks, ignore how persuasive or professional the sentence sounds. Authors routinely dress up a personal belief to make it look official. Train your child to ask: "Could a machine verify this as a data point without any human feelings?"

The Simple Test: "The Sun is classified as a star" (Fact we can prove) vs "The Sun creates a beautiful view" (Opinion based on personal taste).
The Number Trap: "The math book had exactly 42 pages and was far too boring to finish." (Separating a clear numbers fact from a personal feeling of boredom).
The Expert Illusion: "The chief scientist argued that the new rule was a complete failure." (Is the word 'failure' a raw piece of math data, or just the scientist's personal opinion?)

Phase 4: Common Mistake to Watch For

The "Important Expert" Illusion Trap: Children often assume a sentence must be a fact just because it is said by an important character, like a "Professor," "Doctor," or "Explorer" (for example, reading 'The Head Doctor stated that the syrup tasted completely awful' and marking it as a fact).

The Easy Fix: Spot the Value Words. Remind your child of this rule: "Describing words are usually just opinions pretending to be facts." If a word is based on a personal reaction or a score rating (like best, worst, important, or awful), it is always an opinion. Even professional experts have personal opinions!
