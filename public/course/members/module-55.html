Module 55: Fact vs. Opinion

Phase 1: The X-Ray

Think of this as "The Truth Filter." In Reading Comprehension, your child must learn to separate Facts (things that can be proven with a clock, a ruler, or a camera) from Opinions (personal feelings or value judgments). The secret weapon is ignoring the "fancy language." Authors often dress up their opinions in very authoritative words to make them sound like facts. To find the truth, your child must ask: "Could a machine prove this?"

The Direct Proof "The sun is a star." (Fact) vs. "The sun is beautiful." (Opinion). Can we measure beauty?
The Number Trap "The report was 42 pages long and was far too tedious." (Contrast the factual count with the subjective feeling).
The Authority Filter "The world's greatest scientist argued that the policy was a failure." (Is 'failure' a fact just because an expert said it?)

Phase 2: The Anatomy

This is the Measurement Machine. Before your child marks an answer, they should put the statement through this filter. If the output can be recorded as a piece of data, it’s a fact. If the output depends on who is looking at it, it’s an opinion. Help your child see that "stunning" and "difficult" are words that change depending on the person.

Phase 4: The Saboteur

The "Expert Fallacy" Trap: This is the sneakiest trap in the 11+. Children often believe a statement is a fact just because a "Professor" or "Doctor" said it. For example: "The Doctor said the medicine tasted horrible." Because the Doctor is real, they assume "horrible" is a fact.

The Insider Fix: The "Value Word" Audit. Tell your child: "Adjectives are often opinions in disguise." If a word describes a feeling (good, bad, scary, boring) or a value (best, worst, important), it is an opinion—no matter who says it. If you can't prove it with a tool, it remains a belief. Factual people can still have opinions.
