Module 55: Provable Facts vs. Personal Opinions

Foundation Intro

The Ruler vs. The Heart Test

A true Provable Fact represents an objective truth you can easily measure or verify using an external tool, like a physical ruler, a calendar, or a clock. A Personal Opinion simply illustrates how an individual feels inside their own heart. Describing a feeling doesn't mean the statement is automatically "wrong"—it just means an objective tracking tool can't calculate it as data!

"Look closely at this baseline statement: 'The cake is 10cm tall.' Can we deploy a standard ruler tool to confirm that measurement? Yes, we can! That instantly locks it in as a solid fact. But if we pivot and say 'The cake is delicious,' can a ruler prove that calculation? No, it can't! 'Delicious' is a subjective heart-feeling, which classifies the claim as a personal opinion."

Phase 1: Spotting the Pattern

Approach advanced verbal reasoning exercises as executing a rigorous "Linguistic Truth Filter." Inside comprehension excerpts, your student must learn to ruthlessly detach verifiable Facts (metrics that can be cross-checked by physical instruments) from subjective Opinions (value judgments anchored in personal taste). Winning high marks requires ignoring persuasive, decorated prose. Writers routinely coat personal beliefs in authoritative phrasing. Train your child to ask: "Could an automated machine verify this as a data point without human bias?"

The Baseline Verification: "The Sun maps out as a star" (Verifiable Fact) vs "The Sun creates a beautiful morning view" (Subjective Personal Opinion).
The Numerical Context Trap: "The analytical report spanned exactly 42 pages and proved far too tedious to complete." (Detangling a concrete fact from an individual's personal boredom threshold).
The Authoritative Filter: "The senior lead scientist argued the local policy change was a total failure." (Is the token 'failure' a piece of raw math data, or an ideological belief statement?)

Phase 4: Common Mistake to Watch For

The Deceptive "Expert Authority" Fallacy: Children frequently assume a sentence must represent an absolute fact simply because it is delivered by an influential character, such as a "Professor," a "Doctor," or an "Explorer" (for example reading 'The Chief Medical Officer stated the medicine tasted utterly repulsive' and marking the claim as a fact).

The Easy Fix: Execute a Value-Word Linguistic Check. Remind your child of this simple rule: "Descriptive adjectives are often personal opinions masquerading as data lines." If a keyword relates to a personal sensory reaction or a value scale rating (such as best, worst, crucial, or awful), it functions as an opinion. Highly professional experts can still express subjective opinions.
