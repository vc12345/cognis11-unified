Module 11: Fair-Share Speed

Foundation Intro

The Fair-Share Split

Finding a journey's pace over a whole trip isn't about using a rigid equation; it is a simple game of sharing out fairly. Imagine your child has a big card deck of miles. To find the true pace, they just have to deal those miles evenly into separate "Hour Boxes" like playing cards until the pile is completely gone.

"Look at our deck of 40 total miles. We have exactly 2 hours to complete the journey. If we want to be completely fair and travel the exact same length during each individual hour, how many miles must we place into each hour's box?"

The "Single Box" Golden Rule

Remind your child: "The fair-share speed is simply the value packed inside ONE single box." It does not matter how grand the overall road trip totals are; we only want to look at the exact share belonging to one standalone hour.

Foundation Progressing

The Balancing Scale

In 11+ papers, the overall pace of a journey is rarely situated perfectly in the middle of your two speeds. It is affected by gravity. If a vehicle travels quite slowly for 5 hours and sprints quickly for only 1 hour, the final shared pace will be forcefully dragged down toward the slow speed side.

"Our car traveled at two completely different speeds during this trip. But look closely at our balancing weights—we spent significantly more time crawling along slowly. That extra 'Slow Time' acts as a heavier weight, winning the tug-of-war and pulling our final shared pace closer to it."

The "Tie-Breaker" Secret

The only time your final pace sits exactly dead-center between your speeds is if your driving durations are a Perfect Tie. Always hunt for the winner! The speed that commands the most road time will always win the visual pull on our scale.

Phase 1: Spotting the Pattern

Tracking progress over a complex journey requires a Big Picture Perspective. In the 11+, examiners intentionally provide two distinct speeds to tempt your child into a rushed, instinctive guess. The absolute best tool is to completely ignore those specific mid-way speeds and zoom right out. Success is not about finding the midpoint between two numbers; it is about squashing the whole trip into one single "Grand Distance" and one single "Grand Time." Once your child collapses the entire journey into these two Master Totals, the points are instantly theirs.

The Family Road Trip: "A car drives 15 miles in the quiet morning and 25 miles in the busy afternoon. The entire trip took 2 hours. What was the fair-share speed over the whole day?"
The Weekend Cycle Ride: "A cyclist travels 20km at a steady 10km/h pace, then completes another 20km leg at a quicker 20km/h speed. What is the fair-share speed across the whole trip?"
The Helicopter Route: "An aircraft flies 600 miles at 300mph, then completes a 400-mile leg at 200mph. Calculate the shared speed for the combined 1,000-mile flight path."

Phase 4: Common Mistake to Watch For

The "Speed Splitting" Trap: If a vehicle journeys along at 40mph for a while and then speeds up to 60mph, enthusiastic kids will often instantly declare the answer to be "50mph!" It feels completely logical because 50 sits perfectly right in the center. This is a classic 11+ speed trap.

The Insider Solution: Tell your child directly: "The separate speeds on the page do not matter at all until the very final step." You can only calculate the true shared pace by dividing your Master Grand Totals. Unless the exact time spent driving at each speed is a perfect match (which is almost never the case in entrance exams), simply adding the speeds together will run you straight into a wrong answer.
