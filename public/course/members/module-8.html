The Secret Treasure Boxes

When we are sharing out money instead of food, we don't use a kitchen knife. Instead, we divide the total sum into Equal Treasure Chests. To discover 2/5 of £100, we start by splitting our full £100 across 5 individual chests. Once we see how much money sits inside 1 single chest, we just collect the 2 chests we are allowed to take.

"Imagine we have a grand pile of £100. Our target fraction is 2/5, which means we must set up 5 separate treasure boxes. Let's look inside just one single box first—it holds exactly £20! Because our top number tells us to take 2 boxes, we pick up two chests to reveal our final total of £40."

Phase 1: Spotting the Pattern

Every fraction in a word problem boils down to a two-step game: Cut and Collect. The bottom value shows you how many equal blocks to slice your total into. The top value shows you exactly how many of those blocks to scoop up onto your plate. To solve any fraction puzzle under exam pressure, always track down the value of **just one single block** first.

Starting Level: "Find 1/2 of 12 luxury sweets. How many sweets end up in each of the two groups?"
Intermediate Level: "A birthday cake weighs 500g and is cut into 5 equal portions. If 3 portions are eaten, what is the combined mass of the missing cake?"
Advanced Level: "A challenging 40km cross-country race is 5/8 complete. How many kilometers are left to run?"

Phase 4: Common Mistake to Watch For

The "Upside-Down" Trap: Rushed children often try to divide the grand total by the top number simply because it is the very first digit they scan on the page. This leads to confusingly massive answers.

The Easy Fix: The "Knife and Hand" Rule. Remind your child: the bottom number is the Knife (we divide by it). The top number is the Hand (we multiply by it). Always cut down to find one piece first. If the final answer looks larger than the starting amount, your fraction rule has gone upside down!
