The Scissors Cut Rule

When flat shapes overlap on a page, the front shape stays perfectly whole, but it acts like a pair of scissors cutting a bite straight out of the shape underneath! The shape on top always keeps its clean, perfect borders, while the background shape will show a broken outline where it has been "cut."

Phase 1: Spotting the Pattern

Identifying layer order follows a basic strategy: **The Layer Rule**. Test designers deliberately combine shapes to see if your child can find the "last dropped" item. The absolute fastest shortcut is looking for **Line Continuity**. If a shape has a perfectly clean, uncut border, it sits in the top slot. If its border gets interrupted, it is trapped on a deeper back layer.

The Overlapping Pair: A red circle overlaps a blue square. The circle's edge is completely perfect, while the square's outline stops against it. The circle wins the top slot!
The Multi-Shape Pile: When multiple items cross paths, find the one shape that has zero cuts to its borders. That is your definitive starting point for peel-back tracking.
The Hidden Center Shape: A shape that is buried deep underneath other layers will show multiple line cuts and broken borders around its tiny visible patches.

Phase 4: Common Mistake to Watch For

The "Same Color" Camouflage Trap

When two overlapping shapes share the exact same color fill, a child's eyes can blend them into a single big blob. They might miss the fact that one shape is actually standing behind the other.

The Easy Fix: Trace for Missing Bites. Coach your child with this step: "Look for where the borders collide." Ignore the color fills completely and follow the line paths. If Shape A's line stops suddenly or has an entire slice cut out of it by Shape B's rim, Shape A is trapped at the back. The true top shape always keeps its whole uncut border lines!
