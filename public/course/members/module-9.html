Module 9: Fractions of Leftovers

Foundation Intro

The Shrinking Pie

In these two-step challenges, the total pile changes size right in the middle of the story. Once the first group takes their share, the next person only looks at what is **actually left over**. Think of it like taking two distinct trips to the kitchen counter.

"We start off with a whole, uncut pie. First, we take away a portion of it. Now, look at what remains on the tray. If we want to take a fraction of it again, we aren't cutting into the original big total anymore; we are only sharing out that **new, smaller leftover piece**. Watch the pie change sizes step-by-step!"
Foundation Progressing

The Zoom-In Shortcut

To solve these tracking questions easily, imagine placing a clear "Stop Sign" right after the first step. Once you subtract the initial amount, that specific **leftover remainder** acts as your brand-new starting total for the second fraction.

"Look at the top tracking bar. We spent some of our money right away. See the dotted lines pointing downward? They zoom straight in on the remainder. The second bar only cares about that leftover cash space. We never look back at the starting total!"

Phase 1: Spotting the Pattern

This is commonly known in exams as the "Leftovers Trap." The core total drops down right in the middle of the paragraph. Once the first group of items is taken, the next fraction only measures what remains. If your child can focus entirely on tracking the Leftover Amount, they will glide past a classic mistake.

Sweets Sharing: "Sam has 20 fruit sweets. He eats 4 of them. He then gives 1/2 of what is left to Sue. How many sweets remain in the pack?"
Reading Goals: "A storybook has 120 pages. A student reads 1/3 of the book on Monday, and then reads 1/4 of the remaining pages on Tuesday. How many pages are left to read?"
Holiday Budgeting: "You have £400 saved. You spend 2/5 of the money on a hotel room, and 1/3 of the leftovers on travel tickets. What is your final balance?"

Phase 4: Common Mistake to Watch For

The "Original Total" Trap: Most students try to calculate the second fraction step using the original grand total number (for example, attempting to find 2/5 of £600 instead of 2/5 of the actual leftover £450 pile).

The Easy Fix: Circle the Secret Word. Train your child to look out for keywords like "leftovers," "remainder," or "what is left." If they spot them, they must write down a big visual stop sign. They have to run a subtraction step to calculate the new pile size before they allow themselves to touch the second fraction.
