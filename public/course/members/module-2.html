Foundation Intro

The Sweet Sharing Game

A ratio is simply a Sharing Rule. For example, if the rule is 2:1, it means that every single time you put 2 sweets onto the blue plate, you must put 1 sweet onto the gold plate. You repeat this rule over and over until the main pile is empty.

"We have a pile of sweets and a strict sharing rule. Every time I hand out sweets, I must follow the rule perfectly. Let's see how many rounds we can play before our main pile runs out of sweets!"

The Magic Envelopes

When we want to share a large amount of money like £100, we don't hand out individual £1 coins one by one. Instead, we use a clever shortcut: we group the money into Magic Envelopes. First, we figure out how much money fits inside one single envelope, and then we give the envelopes out to match our sharing rule.

"Imagine we want to share money using the rule 3:2. This means one person gets 3 envelopes and the other gets 2 envelopes. That makes 5 envelopes in total. If we divide our whole £100 equally into these 5 envelopes, how much money goes inside each one?"

Phase 1: Spotting the Pattern

The easiest way to solve any exam ratio problem is to find the value of One Single Box. Once your child discovers what a single share or "box" is worth, they can calculate the exact amount for any person in the question with total confidence.

Level 1: Share £20 using the rule 3:1. How much money does the person with the larger share get?
Level 2: A 40cm piece of ribbon is cut into two pieces using the rule 5:3. How much longer is the first piece?
Level 3: Two paths have a length ratio of 2:7. If the total combined distance is 180km, find the length of the shorter path.

Phase 4: Common Mistake to Watch For

The "One-Sided" Mistake: Eager children often take the total amount of money and divide it by just one side of the ratio rule. They forget that the total amount must be shared across the entire group of boxes combined.

The Easy Fix: The "Add First" Rule. Always ask your child directly: "How many total boxes do we have mixed together in our bar model?" Remind them to add the ratio numbers together first.
