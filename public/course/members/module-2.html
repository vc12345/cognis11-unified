Module 2: Shared Ratios

Phase 1: The X-Ray

Think of a ratio as a Chocolate Bar. To share it fairly, you don't just look at two people; you look at the total number of "chunks." The secret weapon for every ratio problem is finding the Value of One Chunk. Once your child knows what one single piece is worth, they can hand out the right amount to anyone.

The Sweet Shop "Share 15 sweets between Ben and Sam in the ratio 2:1. How many does Ben get?"
The Cash Prize "A £60 prize is split in the ratio 5:7. What is the larger share?"
The Ruler Challenge "A 1.5m line is divided 3:2. Find the difference between the two lengths."

Phase 2: The Anatomy

This is the Partition Bar. Every box inside this bar is identical. To find the "Value of One Box," you must divide the Total Amount by the Total Number of Boxes. Point to the bar below—count the boxes together to find the "Total Parts" before you do any math.

Phase 3: The Engine

Adjust the ratio sliders with your child. Watch how the Total Parts change, and see how the "Total Value" is spread across every single box. Point out that when the ratio changes, the "Value of One" has to shift to keep the total the same.

Phase 4: The Saboteur

The "One-Sided" Mistake: Children often try to divide the total by just one side of the ratio (e.g., trying to find one part by doing $£100 \div 3$). They forget that the money covers the whole bar, not just one person's share.

The Fix: The "Add First" Rule. Remind your child: "Before you divide, you must find the Whole Bar." Add the ratio numbers together (A + B) first to see the total number of parts. Only then can you find the value of one chunk.
