The Handshake Staircase

The easiest way to count handshakes is to build a Staircase. The first person meets everyone else. The next person meets everyone who is left. The number of new handshakes goes down by one every time until the last person has already met everyone!

"Look at the staircase for 5 people. Person A makes 4 handshakes. Person B makes 3. Person C makes 2. Person D makes 1. And Person E? They've already met everyone! To find the total, we just add the stairs: 4 + 3 + 2 + 1 = 10."

Phase 1: The X-Ray

Think of this as the "Party Puzzle." A handshake is a two-way street. If you visualize a grid of everyone in the room, you'll see that most handshakes are just mirror images. To find the unique interactions, we must "clean" the grid of self-shaking and duplicates.

The High-Five "4 friends high-five each other once. How many happen in total?"
The Tournament "8 teams. Every team plays every other team once. How many games?"
The Riddle "45 handshakes happened at a meeting. How many people were there?"

Phase 2: The Anatomy

This is the Matrix of Possibility. The diagonal dots are Self-Shakes (impossible). When you filter the grid, exactly half of the squares vanish. Those are the Mirror Images. Shaking hands with Person B is the same event as Person B shaking hands with you.

Phase 3: The Engine

Adjust the number of people. Watch the formula: we multiply people by $(n-1)$ to remove themselves, then divide by 2 to remove mirrors.

THE FORMULA n(n - 1) / 2

Phase 4: The Saboteur

The "Reflection" Trap: For 10 people, many kids calculate $10 \times 9 = 90$ and stop. They are counting every handshake twice!

The Fix: The "Two-to-One" Rule. "It takes two people to make one handshake." Always divide the group pairings by 2. If you don't divide, you are counting hands, not handshakes.
