The Snail's Pace

Speed isn't a complex math calculation; it is really just the physical size of One Single Jump. If a snail has a traveling speed of "3 cm per second," it means that every single time our clock ticks forward, the snail glides exactly 3 cm across the floor.

"Look closely at our little snail below. His speed is currently set to 2 cm per second. Every single time you press the 'Tick' button, he will take exactly one forward jump. Let's count them out loud together: One tick matches 2 cm. Two ticks matches 4 cm. Speed simply means the distance covered in just one step!"

The Big Journey Total

Distance represents our Grand Total. If you already know the Speed (how far you travel in 1 hour) and the total Time (how many hours the journey takes), you simply multiply those two parts together. Think of it like assembling a long road out of identical speed blocks placed end-to-end.

"Imagine we are driving a car at a steady 60 mph. That number represents our block size. If we keep driving along for 3 hours, we just stack three of those 60-mile blocks together on our map. Watch how our distance grows! Distance is the ultimate goal because it combines the other two values together."

Phase 1: Spotting the Pattern

Think of Speed, Distance, and Time as an inseparable Travel Trio. They are simply three sides of the exact same journey story. The best shortcut to solving these problems under exam pressure is recognizing that Distance is the Master Value—it sits right at the top of the pile. Once you see how this trio links up, you can track down any missing piece of a trip in seconds.

The Remote Toy Car: "A small toy car speeds across the playground, covering 20m in exactly 4 seconds. What is its steady speed?"
The Fast Train: "An express train commutes smoothly down a track at 75mph. How far will it travel over 3 hours?"
The Flight Path: "A passenger plane flies a total of 1,200 miles at a speed of 400mph. How long does the flight take?"

Phase 4: Common Mistake to Watch For

The "Clock Confuser" Trap: Bright children often forget that regular clocks and mathematical calculators speak entirely different languages. For a journey that takes 1 hour and 30 minutes, they often erroneously plug 1.3 directly into their formula. This is an exam speed trap!

The Easy Fix: The "Out of 60" Rule. Remind them that minutes are fractions of an hour's full 60 minutes. Therefore, 30 minutes must be written as 0.5 (since 30/60 equals a half). 15 minutes translates to 0.25. Always change your stray minutes into crisp decimals before they touch the math triangle!
