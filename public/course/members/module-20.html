Phase 1: Spotting the Pattern

Think of value swapping as a Recipe Ingredient Swap. You are provided with a blueprint rule (the basic recipe), and you must switch the placeholders out for real quantities. The central trick is remembering that items touching means multiplication. If a letter acts like a beaker container, the value is the numerical fluid we pour inside.

Simple Swap: "If $a=10$ and $b=3$, find the combined value of $a+b$."
Glued Characters: "If $x=5$ and $y=4$, calculate the exact balance of $3x - 2y$."
Advanced Powerplay: "If $p=4$ and $q=9$, track down the output value for $(p^2 + q) \div 5$."

Phase 4: Common Mistake to Watch For

The "Digit Gluing" Illusion: If an exam question states that $A=5$, rushed children look at a term like $3A$ and write down $35$. They are treating our structural letter like an empty space placeholder in a two-digit number!

The Easy Fix: The "Invisible Multiplication" Rule. Remind your child that algebra layouts get tightly packed. If a number and a letter touch with no sign between them, they must multiply. Therefore, $3A$ truly means $3 \times 5 = 15$. Always place safety brackets around your numbers when swapping them into a line.
