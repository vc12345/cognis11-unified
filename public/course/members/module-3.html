Module 3: Ratios with Extra Shares

Foundation Intro

The "Extra Boxes" Game

If a sharing rule says 3:1, it means one person gets 2 more boxes than the other. When a math question tells you the "difference" in money between two people, that extra money belongs completely inside those Extra Boxes. It does not get divided among all the boxes combined!

"We both start with 1 box. But you have 2 extra boxes. If those 2 extra boxes hold £10, then each individual box must hold £5. See how we can look only at the extra boxes to find our answer?"

Foundation Progressing

The Tower Contest

Imagine building two towers with blocks. If Tower A is 15cm taller than Tower B, that 15cm is caused entirely by the extra blocks poking out at the top. To find the height of one single block, we simply divide that 15cm by the number of extra blocks standing above the short tower.

"Look at the red line matching the tops. Tower A is taller by exactly 3 blocks. If this extra space stands for 30cm, how tall is 1 block? We only divide the extra height by the extra blocks—we ignore the rest of the tower for now!"

Phase 1: Spotting the Pattern

In 11+ exams, questions often hide the grand total and only tell you the difference or the gap between two values. Success comes when your child realizes that this specific value belongs entirely inside the Extra Boxes. Once they see that an "extra £20" fits perfectly into "2 extra boxes," they can find the value of a single share immediately.

Pocket Money: "Money is shared in the ratio 3:1. Person A gets £10 more than Person B. How much money does Person B get?"
The Classroom: "The ratio of boys to girls is 5:2. There are 15 more boys than girls on the trip. Find the number of girls."
The Baking Recipe: "The ratio of flour to sugar is 7:3. There is 200g more flour than sugar. Find the total weight of the mix."

Phase 4: Common Mistake to Watch For

The "Add Everything" Mistake: Children often see a number in a word problem and automatically add the ratio parts together (doing 5 + 3 = 8 parts). This will cause a wrong answer if the number given is actually an extra amount rather than a total.

The Easy Fix: The "Subtract First" Rule. If the question uses clues like "more than," "fewer than," or "the difference is," you must subtract the ratio numbers first. The extra money or items can only fit into the extra boxes.
