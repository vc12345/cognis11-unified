Module 1: Finding the Price of One

Foundation Intro

The "Stop at One" Bridge

When a math problem tells us the price of a group of items and asks for a new total, kids can get stuck. The easiest way to solve this is to use a bridge. We do not jump straight to the final answer. First, we stop in the middle to find the value of just one single item (we shrink it). Then, we multiply to find the new total (we grow it).

"If we know how much 4 sweets cost, it is hard to guess how much 10 sweets cost right away. It is too big a jump! Instead, let's look at our bridge. First, let's find the cost of just 1 sweet. Once we know the price of one, we can easily find the price for any number of sweets we want!"

The "No Jumping" Rule

Remind your child: "Always find the value of one first." If they try to guess the final answer without finding the price of a single item, they are trying to leap over the river instead of walking safely across the bridge!

Foundation Progressing

The Smart Shopper

We use this exact same method when we want to compare two different deals at the supermarket. By finding the cost of one single item in each shop, we can easily see which deal saves us the most money.

"Shop A sells a large pack of items, and Shop B sells a small pack. It can be tricky to see which is a better deal just by looking at the total price. But let's look at our scale. If we find the price of just one item in each shop, we can quickly see which shop is charging us less for the exact same thing."

Phase 1: Spotting the Pattern

This section focuses on the "Find One First" technique. In 11+ exams, children often feel overwhelmed when they see word problems with several numbers. Instead of guessing how the numbers connect, teach them to always stop at a middle step: find the weight, cost, or distance for just one item. Once they hold the key for a single item, they can scale it up to match any target the exam question asks for.

The Sweet Shop: "If 4 apples cost 80p, how much do 6 apples cost?"
The Road Trip: "A car travels 150 miles using 5 gallons of fuel. How far can it go using 8 gallons?"
The Heavy Crate: "9 identical boxes weigh 108kg. What is the total mass of 13 boxes?"

Help your child notice the steady flow: we shrink down to find one, and then we grow up to find the final total.

Phase 4: Common Mistake to Watch For

The "Upside-Down Division": Bright children often try to divide the smaller number by the larger number (like trying to do 5 items divided by £20) simply because it feels more familiar to divide that way. This leaves them with an incorrect decimal that does not make sense in a real shop.

The Easy Fix: "Money is Boss." Tell your child: "The total amount (the money or the total weight) always goes into the calculation first." We always divide the Total Value by the Quantity. If they accidentally get a tiny answer like £0.25 for a large cake that clearly costs much more, ask them directly: "Does that price make sense in a real shop?"
