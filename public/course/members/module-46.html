Phase 1: Spotting the Pattern

Think of sequential design progressions as working through a Film Strip Series. Your main strategy is finding the matching mathematical rule that governs the change from box to box. If a dot takes a single step right in frame one, it *must* take that exact same step right in the next frames. Train your child to trace just one tiny part of the layout at a time to shield themselves from confusion.

The Straight Step: A circle jumps from top-left straight to bottom-right across the grid. Where must it go in the final frame box?
The Double Rule: A shape spins exactly 90 degrees clockwise AND swaps its color from dark to light on every single step.
The Triple Threat: An object changes its location, its color fill, and its size all at once. Trace each profile property completely on its own!

Phase 4: Common Mistake to Watch For

The "Whole Picture Guessing" Trap: Children trying to save time often try to guess the answer by loosely staring at the entire picture frame all at once. Their brain gets flooded by all the different moving parts, causing them to miss the exact stepping rules entirely.

The Easy Fix: Divide and Conquer. Give your child this simple rule: "Hide the noise, solve the piece." Use a finger or index scratch card to cover up everything in the box except for one single shape feature. Solve that shape's target destination first. Then, cover it up and move directly to the next item. Solving three tiny puzzles one-by-one is much easier than tackling one giant storm!
