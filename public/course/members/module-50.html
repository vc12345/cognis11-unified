Phase 1: Spotting the Pattern

Approach compound punch layout puzzles as mastering "Mental Reverse Mirroring." Arriving at the correct solution quickly depends entirely on tracing the unfolding steps in absolute reverse order. Every single crease line functions as a perfect axis of symmetry. If a hole exists on one flank of a fold, a duplicate must project onto the opposing flank. Trace these independent twin pairs step-by-step until your blueprint model lies flat on the page.

The Single Split Fold: 1 simple fold, followed by 1 punch. Trace out the balanced mirror symmetry across the central vertical axis line.
The Quarter Corner Grid: 2 perpendicular folds, followed by 2 distinct punches. Project matches across the horizontal and vertical axes simultaneously (yielding 8 holes total).
The Eleven-Plus Master Challenge: 3 overlapping folds. Dissect compound radial reflections where geometric shapes seem to twist and step across triangular layouts.

Phase 4: Common Mistake to Watch For

The "Sliding Shift" Illusion Trap: Children frequently look at a paper fragment and assume a hole simply slides across into the next quadrant while keeping its exact original placement orientation (such as assuming a punch nestled in the upper-left corner of a small scrap stays in the upper-left zone of the big open sheet).

The Easy Fix: Enforce the Balanced Crease Distance Habit. Coach your student with this exact visual trick: "The fold acts like an absolute magnet." If a punched hole rests precisely 10mm away from the crease line on the source panel, its clone must map out exactly 10mm away from that crease line on the flipped side. Always mirror the distance away from the fold, never the general position on the grid.
