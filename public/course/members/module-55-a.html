Module 55: Fact vs. Opinion

Foundation Intro

The Ruler vs. The Heart

A **Fact** is something you can measure with a tool, like a ruler or a clock. An **Opinion** is how someone feels in their heart. It doesn't mean it's "wrong," it just means a machine can't prove it!

"Look at this statement: **'The cake is 10cm tall.'** Can we use a ruler to prove that? Yes! That makes it a Fact. But if we say **'The cake is delicious,'** can a ruler prove that? No! Delicious is a heart-feeling, which makes it an Opinion."

The Machine Audit

In the 11+, authors use "Fancy Words" to make opinions sound like facts. To catch them, you must perform a **Machine Audit**. If a robot could record it as data, it passes. If a robot would be confused, it fails.

"Look at this sentence: 'The **difficult** exam started at **9:00 AM**.' Click 'Audit' to see the machine work. '9:00 AM' passes because a clock can prove it. 'Difficult' fails because some people might find it easy! Adjectives are usually opinions in disguise."

Phase 1: The X-Ray

Think of this as "The Truth Filter." In Reading Comprehension, you must separate Facts (measurable by tools) from Opinions (personal value judgments). Success relies on ignoring "fancy language." Authors dress up opinions in authoritative words. Always ask: "Could a machine prove this?"

Direct Proof "Sun is a star" (Fact) vs "Sun is beautiful" (Opinion).
The Number Trap "The report was 42 pages long and far too tedious." (Fact vs Subjective feeling).
Authority Filter "The scientist argued the policy was a failure." (Is 'failure' data or a belief?)

Phase 2: The Anatomy

The Measurement Machine. If the output of a sentence can be recorded as data, it's a fact. If it depends on the observer's perspective, it's an opinion. Note that "stunning" or "standard" vary by person.

Phase 4: The Saboteur

The "Expert Fallacy" Trap: Children often believe a statement is a fact just because a "Professor" or "Doctor" said it. (e.g. "The Doctor said it tasted horrible").

The Fix: The "Value Word" Audit. "Adjectives are often opinions in disguise." If a word describes a *feeling* or a *value* (best, worst, important), it is an opinion. **Factual people can still have opinions.**
